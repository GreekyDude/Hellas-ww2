1940-1941: The Italian War

Greece's Heroic Stand: The First Allied Victory Against Axis Powers

When Mussolini's forces invaded on October 28, 1940, Greece's unexpected resistance became a pivotal moment that changed the course of World War II.

The Italian Invasion of Greece

On October 28, 1940, after the Greek Prime Minister Ioannis Metaxas rejected an Italian ultimatum demanding the occupation of Greek territory, Italian forces invaded Greece from neighboring Albania. This date is now celebrated in Greece as "Oxi Day" ("No" Day), commemorating Metaxas's alleged response to the ultimatum.

Italian forces marching toward the Greek border through Albanian mountains, October 1940.

Greek Counteroffensive

Despite being vastly outnumbered and outgunned, the Greek forces mounted a successful defense and then a counteroffensive that drove the Italians back into Albania. By mid-December 1940, Greek forces had captured the strategic Albanian towns of Korçë, Sarandë, and Gjirokastër, and were threatening Vlorë, a major Italian supply base.

"Hence we will not say that Greeks fight like heroes, but that heroes fight like Greeks."

— Winston Churchill, 1941

The Greek victory over the Italians was the first Allied land victory of World War II and helped raise morale across occupied Europe. It forced Hitler to delay his invasion of the Soviet Union to secure his southern flank by invading Greece, a decision that would prove costly when German forces faced the Russian winter.

The Battle of Pindus

The Battle of Pindus was the first major engagement of the Greco-Italian War. The Italian Julia Alpine Division, elite mountain troops, attempted to advance through the Pindus Mountains toward Metsovo to cut Greek forces in half. They were met with fierce resistance from the Greek 4th Infantry Division and local civilians.

Greek soldiers defending positions in the snow-covered Pindus Mountains, November 1940.

The Role of Civilian Volunteers

One of the most remarkable aspects of the Greek defense was the role played by civilian volunteers, particularly women from local villages. These women, known as the "women of Pindus," carried ammunition, food, and supplies to the front lines and evacuated the wounded, often under enemy fire.

By November 13, 1940, the Julia Division was encircled and forced to retreat with heavy losses. The Greek victory at Pindus was a significant blow to Italian morale and demonstrated the effectiveness of Greek mountain warfare tactics against a numerically superior force.
